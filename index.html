Failure Amplification

How small problems become big outcomes — by compounding, coupling, and delay.

Concept Scenario Principles

A short, self-contained explanation

Failure amplification is when a small failure doesn’t stay small.

In many systems, the disaster isn’t a single catastrophic event. It’s a chain reaction: minor issues interact, spread, and compound until the system can’t recover in time.

Working definition

Failure amplification is the process where initially manageable problems grow in impact because of feedback loops, hidden coupling, and delayed detection.

Why it’s easy to miss

Small failures often look harmless in isolation. The risk lives in the interaction between failures: one workaround creates pressure elsewhere, which creates another workaround, and so on.

What this project does

It keeps scope narrow: one core idea, one concrete example, and a readable format. The aim is to make the pattern visible, not to overwhelm you with theory.

What you should gain

A better instinct for when “minor” issues are actually early signals of cascading failure — and what conditions make cascades more likely.

Principles that drive amplification

1) Delay hides damage

If consequences arrive late, you keep doing the thing that caused them. By the time the signal appears, the system has already accumulated debt.

2) Coupling spreads impact

Tightly connected parts share failure. One component running “a little hot” becomes everyone’s problem when there’s no slack.

3) Workarounds create new risk

A workaround can be rational in the moment — but it often moves risk to a place you’re not watching, and makes the next failure more likely.

4) Feedback loops accelerate

Once pressure creates behaviors that increase pressure, the system enters a loop. Loops are where “small” becomes “unstoppable.”
